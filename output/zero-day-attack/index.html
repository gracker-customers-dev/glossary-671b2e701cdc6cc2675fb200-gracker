Zero-day attack

A cyber attack that occurs on the same day a vulnerability is discovered in software, before a fix is available.

Description

A zero-day attack exploits a previously unknown vulnerability in software or hardware that developers have not yet had the opportunity to patch. The term 'zero-day' refers to the fact that the developers have had zero days to address the flaw, leaving users exposed to potential exploitation. These attacks can be particularly dangerous because they can occur before security measures have been implemented, allowing attackers to gain unauthorized access to systems, steal data, or deploy malware. Zero-day vulnerabilities are often highly sought after by cybercriminals and can be used in targeted attacks against organizations or individuals. The impact of a zero-day attack can be severe, depending on the nature of the vulnerability and the information or systems that are compromised. Organizations often invest in threat detection and incident response capabilities to mitigate the risks associated with zero-day attacks and to respond quickly when new vulnerabilities are discovered.

Examples

  • The Stuxnet worm exploited multiple zero-day vulnerabilities in Windows to target industrial control systems.
  • The recent attack on a popular web browser utilized a zero-day vulnerability to deliver malware to users.

Additional Information

  • Zero-day vulnerabilities are often sold on the black market for significant sums of money.
  • Organizations can use threat intelligence services to stay informed about newly discovered vulnerabilities and potential zero-day threats.
