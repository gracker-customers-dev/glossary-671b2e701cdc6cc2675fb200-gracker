Trojan

A type of malicious software that disguises itself as legitimate software.

Description

In network security, a Trojan, or Trojan horse, is a type of malware that misleads users about its true intent. Unlike viruses or worms, Trojans do not replicate themselves but rely on users to execute them. They can be embedded in software downloads, email attachments, or even legitimate programs. Once activated, Trojans can perform a variety of harmful activities, such as stealing personal information, installing additional malware, or giving unauthorized access to attackers. Trojans often exploit social engineering tactics, convincing users that they are installing a beneficial program. This can lead to significant data breaches and compromise the integrity of personal and organizational networks. Some common types of Trojans include backdoor Trojans, which create a hidden entry point for attackers, and banking Trojans, which target financial information. Protecting against Trojans involves using up-to-date antivirus software, avoiding downloading untrusted applications, and maintaining good security awareness practices.

Examples

  • Zeus Trojan: Targets banking credentials and is known for its stealthy approach.
  • Remote Access Trojans (RATs): Allow attackers to control infected systems remotely.

Additional Information

  • Trojans can be disguised as games, utilities, or even security software.
  • Regular system updates and security patches can help mitigate the risks of Trojan infections.
