Advanced Encryption Standard (AES)

AES is a symmetric encryption algorithm widely used for securing data.

Description

The Advanced Encryption Standard (AES) is a symmetric encryption algorithm established by the U.S. National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) in 2001. It is designed to secure sensitive but unclassified material by encrypting data into an unreadable format using a secret key. AES operates on fixed block sizes of 128 bits and supports key sizes of 128, 192, or 256 bits, providing varying levels of security. The algorithm involves several rounds of processing, including substitution, permutation, and mixing of the input data, which enhances its resistance to various forms of cryptanalysis. Due to its efficiency and security, AES is widely adopted across various applications, including securing data at rest, transmitting data over networks, and encrypting communications in protocols such as SSL/TLS and VPNs. AES is considered one of the most secure encryption standards today and is endorsed by government and industry standards worldwide.

Examples

  • AES is used in securing Wi-Fi networks through WPA2 and WPA3 protocols.
  • AES encryption is commonly utilized for encrypting files and data in cloud storage services.

Additional Information

  • AES has been extensively analyzed and is widely regarded as secure against all known practical attacks.
  • The algorithm is implemented in various programming languages and is a fundamental part of modern cryptographic systems.
