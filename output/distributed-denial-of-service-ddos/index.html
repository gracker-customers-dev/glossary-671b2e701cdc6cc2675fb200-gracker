Distributed Denial of Service (DDoS)

A type of cyber attack that aims to make a service unavailable by overwhelming it with traffic from multiple sources.

Description

A Distributed Denial of Service (DDoS) attack is a malicious attempt to disrupt the normal functioning of a targeted server, service, or network by overwhelming it with a flood of Internet traffic. DDoS attacks typically involve multiple compromised computer systems, often part of a botnet, which are used to send large volumes of requests to the target. This flood of incoming traffic can exhaust the resources of the target, causing it to slow down significantly or become completely unresponsive. DDoS attacks can vary in size and sophistication, ranging from simple attacks that utilize a small number of compromised systems to complex multi-vector attacks that leverage hundreds or thousands of bots. These attacks can be motivated by various factors, including political agendas, extortion, or simply mischief. Organizations often implement various security measures, such as traffic filtering, rate limiting, and DDoS protection services, to mitigate the impact of such attacks.

Examples

  • The 2016 Dyn attack that disrupted major websites like Twitter and Netflix.
  • The 2000 Mafiaboy attack that brought down several high-profile websites, including CNN and eBay.

Additional Information

  • DDoS attacks can be classified into three main types: volumetric attacks, protocol attacks, and application layer attacks.
  • Mitigating DDoS attacks often requires a combination of technologies and strategies, including the use of content delivery networks (CDNs) and specialized DDoS mitigation services.
