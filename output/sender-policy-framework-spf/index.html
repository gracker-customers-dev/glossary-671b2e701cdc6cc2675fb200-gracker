Sender Policy Framework (SPF)

SPF is an email authentication protocol designed to detect forging sender addresses during the delivery of the email.

Description

Sender Policy Framework (SPF) is an email authentication method that allows domain owners to specify which mail servers are permitted to send email on behalf of their domain. By adding an SPF record to the Domain Name System (DNS), it creates a list of IP addresses that are authorized to send emails for that domain. When an email is received, the receiving mail server checks the SPF record of the sender's domain to determine if the email is coming from an authorized server. If the sending server's IP address is not listed in the SPF record, the email can be flagged as potential spam or rejected altogether. This helps to prevent email spoofing, which is a common tactic in phishing attacks. SPF works in conjunction with other email authentication methods like DKIM (DomainKeys Identified Mail) and DMARC (Domain-based Message Authentication, Reporting & Conformance) to provide a layered approach to email security, ensuring that the integrity and authenticity of emails are maintained.

Examples

  • A company configures its SPF record to allow only its own mail server to send emails, preventing unauthorized use of its domain.
  • An organization receives an email from a domain that has an SPF record, and the sending server's IP matches the record, validating the email's authenticity.

Additional Information

  • SPF records are published as TXT records in the DNS.
  • SPF does not encrypt emails; it only provides a way to authenticate the sender.
