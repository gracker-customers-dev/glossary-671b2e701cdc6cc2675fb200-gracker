FISMA

The Federal Information Security Management Act is a U.S. law aimed at protecting government information and information systems.

Description

The Federal Information Security Management Act (FISMA), enacted in 2002 and updated in 2014, is a United States federal law that requires federal agencies to secure their information systems. FISMA mandates that these agencies develop, document, and implement an information security program to protect sensitive data from unauthorized access, use, disclosure, disruption, modification, or destruction. The law emphasizes the importance of risk management and continuous monitoring of information systems. FISMA requires that agencies adhere to standards set by the National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST), including the development of security controls and assessments. The act also calls for regular reporting on the security status of federal information systems to ensure accountability and improve overall security posture. FISMA applies to all federal agencies and their contractors, making it a critical aspect of federal cybersecurity efforts.

Examples

  • A federal agency conducts annual FISMA compliance assessments to ensure their cybersecurity measures are effective.
  • Contractors working with government agencies must comply with FISMA regulations to protect sensitive information.

Additional Information

  • FISMA is part of a larger framework of federal cybersecurity efforts, including the Cybersecurity Framework and the Federal Risk and Authorization Management Program (FedRAMP).
  • Failure to comply with FISMA can result in penalties for federal agencies, including potential loss of funding.
